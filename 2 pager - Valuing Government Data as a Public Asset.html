Research Proposal

Valuing Government Data in Queensland

Date: September 2025

1. Introduction

This proposal outlines a framework for valuing government data assets within the Queensland public sector. Data has become a core component of modern governance and service delivery, yet traditional economic valuation methods often fail to capture its full social, ethical, and environmental value. This deficiency leads to suboptimal investment decisions, under-resourced projects, and a lack of accountability for data-driven initiatives.

We propose to adapt and apply a Social Cost-Benefit Analysis (SCBA) framework, enhanced with non-market valuation techniques, to provide a more holistic and accurate method for assessing data project viability. This research will culminate in a practical, user-friendly digital toolkit for public servants to use in their daily work.

2. The Problem

Public sector decisions are often based on financial metrics like return on investment (ROI) that struggle to account for intangible benefits. Data projects, while potentially transformative, often have their primary value in non-monetary outcomes such as improved public safety, environmental protection, or increased citizen engagement.

The absence of a standardised valuation methodology for these benefits makes it difficult to secure funding and demonstrate the true impact of data initiatives, leading to a systemic undervaluation of government data.

3. Proposed Methodology

Our primary methodology will be a SCBA. This approach extends traditional financial analysis by quantifying and including all social, economic, environmental, and ethical impacts of a project, regardless of whether they have a direct market price. 3.1 Non-Market Valuation Techniques

To assign a tangible value to non-financial benefits, we will incorporate three established non-market valuation methods:

Contingent Valuation

Using surveys and direct questioning to determine citizens' willingness-to-pay for benefits derived from data (e.g., enhanced public health services).

Hedonic Pricing

Inferring the value of data by analysing how a data-driven benefit (e.g., a new public dashboard) affects the price of a related market good.

Travel Cost Method

Estimating the value of a data-enabled public service (e.g., a digital map of natural attractions) by calculating the travel costs people incur to use it.

Additionally, we will explore the integration of Real Options Valuation (ROV) to score the value of flexibility and uncertainty inherent in data projects. This will allow the framework to account for future scalability and adaptability.

4. The Digital Toolkit

To ensure the framework is practical and accessible, we will develop a simple digital tool. This toolkit will guide users through a five-step process: (1) Project Details, (2) Costs, (3) Benefits, (4) ROV Scoring, and (5) Final Analysis. This will provide a weighted score for project viability, giving public servants a powerful tool to justify and prioritise data initiatives.

5. Expected Outcomes

This research and the resulting toolkit will deliver several key benefits to the Queensland public sector:

  • A standardised, repeatable methodology for data valuation.
  • The ability to make more comprehensive, data-driven decisions that account for social value.
  • Increased accountability and transparency in data project management.
