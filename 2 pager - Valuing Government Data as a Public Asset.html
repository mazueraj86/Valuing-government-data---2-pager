Research Proposal

Valuing Government Data in Queensland

Date: September 2025

1. Introduction

This proposal outlines a framework for valuing government data assets within the Queensland public sector. Data has become a core component of modern governance and service delivery, yet traditional economic valuation methods often fail to capture its full social, ethical, and environmental value. This deficiency leads to suboptimal investment decisions, under-resourced projects, and a lack of accountability for data-driven initiatives.

We propose to adapt and apply a Social Cost-Benefit Analysis (SCBA) framework, enhanced with non-market valuation techniques, to provide a more holistic and accurate method for assessing data project viability. This research will culminate in a practical, user-friendly digital toolkit for public servants to use in their daily work.

2. The Problem

Public sector decisions are often based on financial metrics that fail to account for the unique, intangible benefits of data-driven initiatives.

The absence of a standardised valuation methodology for these benefits makes it difficult to secure funding and demonstrate the true impact of data projects. This is further complicated by the need to quantify and account for data-specific risks, including privacy, security, and ethical impacts.

3. Proposed Research Methodology

Framework Design

The study adapts SCBA to the specific properties of government data. The framework combines economic evaluation, non-market valuation, and financial modelling to capture both direct and indirect benefits. It also remains sensitive to emerging uses of data such as AI.

Core Analytical Components

Initially the adapted SCBA will integrate five key elements:

Direct Benefits and Costs

Quantify efficiency gains, service improvements, and avoided costs using standard accounting methods.

Spillovers and Innovation Multipliers

Use econometric techniques to identify wider economic effects and innovation triggered by data release.

Option Value

Apply Real Options Valuation (ROV) to estimate the long-term, non-rival potential of high-value datasets.

Privacy, Trust, and Distributional Impacts

Monetise or sensitivity-test potential negative externalities, such as the costs of a data breach.

Governance Multiplier

Develop a composite index to adjust valuations for data quality, accessibility, licensing, and stewardship.

3.2 Case Studies

Stage 1: Proof-of-Concept: Queensland Open Data Portal

Scope: Focus on developing and testing the core SCBA framework using a use case associated with publishing of a representative sample of datasets to the Queensland Open Data Portal. The goal is to establish a replicable toolkit for measuring data's value.

Actions

The research will proceed with the following actions:

Valuing Direct Benefits and Costs

Direct costs (e.g., data collection, cleaning, and hosting) will be calculated using standard accounting methods. Direct benefits will be quantified through surveys and interviews with known data consumers to measure efficiency gains, avoided costs, and time savings.

Quantifying Spillovers

Econometric analysis will identify the causal impact of data releases by tracking economic indicators before and after publication. This will estimate the innovation multiplier and measure value generated beyond direct use.

Estimating Option Value

Real Options Valuation (ROV) will be applied to treat high-value datasets as "real options" that enable future innovation. Sensitivity testing will illustrate the range of possible valuations.

Stage 2: Expansion to Key Agency Datasets (e.g., Procurement Data)

Rationale: Building on the Stage 1 framework, this stage will apply the methodology to more complex, high-value datasets held by government agencies, starting with procurement records.

Benefits of open procurement data, such as increased market competition, anti-corruption, and local supplier participationâ€”will be quantified using a combination of methods:

  • Revealed Preference Methods: Analysing contract data to measure the impact of transparency on bid prices and the number of competing suppliers.
  • Stated Preference Methods: Using choice experiments and contingent valuation surveys with businesses and the public to place a monetary value on the benefits of increased transparency and trust.

The Governance Multiplier: To account for the critical role of data stewardship, a composite governance index will be developed. This index will score datasets on a scale based on key criteria:

  • Data quality: Adherence to the FAIR Principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable).
  • Privacy and trust: The robustness of anonymization, security protocols, and ethical governance will be assessed.
  • Risk: The potential costs of a data breach, including reputational damage and legal fees, will be estimated and incorporated as a negative externality in the SCBA. This multiplier will be used to adjust the overall net social benefit, making the valuation sensitive to the quality of data governance.

The development of this index will be a co-designed process validated through stakeholder workshops to ensure the criteria are transparent, justifiable, and not subjectively weighted.

3.3 Cross-cutting features

Across both stages the framework will consistently:

  • Capture direct benefits and costs using non-market valuation where appropriate.
  • Quantify spillovers and innovation multipliers with robust statistical controls.
  • Estimate option value through ROV.
  • Incorporate privacy, trust, and distributional impacts via sensitivity analysis.
  • Apply the FAIR-based governance multiplier to ensure results reflect data-stewardship quality.

4. The Digital Toolkit

To ensure the framework is practical and accessible, we will develop a simple digital tool. This holistic toolkit will guide users through a five-step process: (1) Project Details & Scope, (2) Costs (Financial & Non-Financial), (3) Benefits (Financial & Non-Financial), (4) ROV & Risk Scoring, and (5) Holistic Viability Assessment. This will provide a weighted score for project viability, giving public servants a powerful tool to justify and prioritise data initiatives.

5. Expected Outcomes

This research and the resulting toolkit will deliver several key benefits to the Queensland public sector:

  • A standardised, repeatable methodology for data valuation.
  • The ability to make more comprehensive, data-driven decisions that account for social value.
  • Increased accountability and transparency in data project management.
